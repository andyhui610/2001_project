# Shots #227 to #234 Audiovisual Analysis: Discovery Centrifuge Jogging & Space Banalization

This analysis note integrates shots #227 to #234 into a group study. This sequence shows astronaut Frank Poole performing a 360-degree, upside-down jog and shadow boxing training inside the giant rotating centrifuge of the spacecraft *Discovery One*. Here, Kubrick creates one of the most famous visual spectacles in film history, not for the sake of showing off special effects, but to utilize an extreme aesthetic of "space normalization" and "de-mythologization," profoundly revealing the monotony of time and the spiritual alienation of the human body during long-distance space travel.

## 1. Composition & Cinematography
* **Rotating Centrifuge & Perspective Illusion**:
  * The camera is firmly fixed to the central axis of the rotating centrifuge. As the massive 38-foot diameter wheel cabin rotates physically at approximately 3 revolutions per minute, the camera remains stationary relative to the cabin wall (or rotates in synchronization with the wheel). This shooting technique presents the runway on screen as a closed, perfect circle.
  * Starting in Shot #227, Poole jogs from a long shot along the curved runway toward the camera. As he advances, his body angle relative to the screen's gravitational orientation continuously changes. When he reaches the top of the screen, he is jogging completely upside down before running down the other side and returning to the bottom of the cabin. Kubrick exploits this unprecedented "vertical inversion" to shatter the viewer's accustomed terrestrial horizon, defining the spatial geometry of artificial gravity in a purely visual language.
  * Deep focus photography with a wide-angle lens clearly captures all details within the centrifuge—including the hibernaculums embedded in the walls, control panels, and light fixtures. Poole remains at the geometric center of this giant circular ring, forming a highly symmetrical, mechanical, and cold one-point perspective that imprisons the human body within a steel cage meticulously designed by industrial engineering.
* **The Mechanics of the Centrifuge Set**:
  * This massive rotating centrifuge set was a physical wheel constructed by Vickers-Armstrongs at a cost of $750,000 commissioned by Kubrick. The actor Gary Lockwood was actually running at the absolute bottom of the wheel (the lowest point of physical gravity) while the colossal steel structure slowly rotated, continuously feeding the runway beneath his feet.
  * To prevent visual anomalies and demonstrate a stunning spatial realism, all control panel instruments, TV screens, and lights had to be physically powered using slip rings, and both the camera and light sources had to be rigorously secured to prevent unnatural vibrations during rotation. This extreme pursuit of physical accuracy provides the imagery with an impeccable, documentary-like sense of realism (*cinéma vérité* of space).

## 2. Color & Lighting
* **Aseptic White Grid Lighting**:
  * Embedded in the centrifuge walls are numerous evenly distributed white latex grid light panels, providing uniform, shadowless, and dead-angle-free diffused light. This aseptic, bright, and temperatureless cold white light completely eliminates any expressionistic shadows, presenting a highly rationalized, scientific, and "fully enlightened and illuminated" modern technological space.
  * This lighting design makes the ship's interior look like a precise biological laboratory. Rather than conquerors of the cosmos, humans inside seem more like experimental specimens placed in a sterile Petri dish, having their physiological metrics monitored.
* **Chromatic Instrumental Glow & HAL's Red Eye**:
  * Amidst the large areas of white and grey tones, tiny colored signal lights (blue, yellow, green) flash on the control panels, adding a faint spark of technological life to the icy, sterile environment.
  * Notably, HAL 9000's iconic red optical lens is silently embedded at several key corners of the centrifuge track. Against the uniform, bright cold light background, this splash of vivid, deep "warning red" stands as the only warm tone, yet it radiates a sense of danger and omnipresent surveillance, foreshadowing the machine's seamless infiltration of human life.

## 3. Sound & Music
* **Absence of Music & Acoustic Realism**:
  * Kubrick uses absolutely no orchestral or electronic music here. This nearly three-minute jog is fully immersed in the physical acoustic environment of the spacecraft interior.
  * The baseline sound on the audio track is the very low-frequency engine noise, air circulation, and the hum of life support systems (ambient hum/system white noise). This deep, continuous, and hypnotic drone acts as the spaceship's only physiological breath, acousticizing the oppression and loneliness of long-distance space voyages.
* **Diegetic Footsteps & Breathing SFX**:
  * Against the low-frequency hum, the "patter-patter" of Poole's running shoes striking the metal floor is clearly defined. Due to the circular geometry of the steel centrifuge, the footsteps produce a slight reverberation and echo in the hollow metallic cavity.
  * As the duration of his jog increases, his breathing becomes heavy and rapid. This physiological gasp of mortal flesh stands in sharp, cruel contrast to the ceaseless, tireless mechanical operation of the ship's engines. Kubrick utilizes these sounds of daily exercise to "de-heroize" the astronauts, emphasizing that they remain mortal beings bound by physiological limits such as muscle atrophy and gravity loss, relying on mechanical daily exercise to maintain their vital functions.

## 4. Editing & Script Adaptation
* **Comparison with Script Page 33**:
  * **Original Script**: In the original script, this scene is described very briefly and is filled with mundane interactions: `We see the rotating hub... Poole secures some loose gear. Poole looks up to TV monitor lens and waves. ... Bowman stops rotation and moves to Entry Port... descending ladder.`
  * **Kubrick's Mundane Editing & Long Takes**: In the final cut, Kubrick completely excises Poole's warm, communicative gesture of waving at the TV monitor, and significantly extends the long takes of jogging and shadow boxing. This jogging sequence serves no narrative function; it is a pure exhibition of the passage of time.
  * **The Satirical Intent of Masterful Editing**: Kubrick transforms a segment that originally possessed narrative functions (Bowman stopping the rotation, descending the ladder) into a wordless, mechanical "time ritual." Poole repeatedly punches (shadow boxing), yet he fights not a physical foe, but the endless void, boredom, and silence of long-distance space travel. This editing arrangement of performing primitive physical actions within a hyper-technological space satirizes how human spiritual life sinks into a boring and repetitive mechanical routine even after mastering the ultimate technology to traverse the solar system.
