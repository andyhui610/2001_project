# Shots #084 to #103 Audiovisual Analysis: Carnivorous Feast & The First Murder

This analysis note conducts a grouped study of shots #084 to #103. This sequence presents the evolutionary practice of Moonwatcher's tribe, transitioning from prey to predators after mastering tools as weapons. They gorge on raw meat in the wilderness, their bodies and fur growing full and sleek. Subsequently, they confront the rival tribe again at the watering hole. This time, Moonwatcher, armed with a heavy femur club, actively wades across the stream and kills the rival's new leader, One-Ear, in the water. This sequence depicts the first instance of "murder of one's own kind using tools" by human ancestors, serving as the ultimate demonstration of the evolution of violence and weaponry.

## 1. Composition & Cinematography
* **Carnivorous Feasting & Relaxed Postures (Static Medium Shots)**:
  * The camera employs static medium shots showing the hominids gathered on the sandy ground scattered with white bones, tearing and chewing large chunks of raw meat with their hands. In contrast to their previous miserable, curled-up postures of digging the ground for roots, the hominids' postures here appear relaxed and arrogant, dominating the frame and visually declaring that they have successfully stood at the top of the food chain.
* **Aggressive Stream Crossing**:
  * During the watering hole conflict, members of Moonwatcher's tribe, wielding bone clubs, cross the stream in a line. The camera uses a horizontal medium shot to capture their steady, oppressive strides as they wade through the water. This composition of actively crossing a spatial barrier demonstrates the awakening of territorial expansion and offensive intent.
* **The High-angle Strike (Reversion of Angle in Tool-based Kill)**:
  * As Moonwatcher swings his club to strike One-Ear, the camera rapidly cuts to a close-up. One-Ear is knocked down into the shallow water, and Moonwatcher towers over him. The camera uses a high-angle shot to show the brutal action of repeatedly swinging the bone club. Water splashes violently in front of the lens, and the sequence settles on a high-angle shot looking down at One-Ear dead in the water, visualizing the absolute dominance of tool-based violence over the physical body.

## 2. Color & Lighting
* **Flesh Red & Bone White Contrast (Bloody Juxtaposition)**:
  * In the eating scene, the dark red of the raw meat stands out starkly and glaringly against the dry, yellowish-brown wasteland background. Paired with the pale animal bones scattered around, this color design dispenses with any warmth, rendering the bloody and cruel essence of evolution in the most naked way.
* **Harsh Noonlight & Shattered Reflections**:
  * The watering hole combat occurs under direct, dry overhead noonlight. The water surface reflects a blinding sky. As the bone club strikes down heavily, it kicks up bright, white splashes, creating stark visual pauses in the frame. After One-Ear falls, the reflection of his black fur in the water is shattered by blood and ripples. The chaotic refraction of light and shadow symbolizes the absolute collapse of the old primeval order.

## 3. Sound & Music Design
* **Cold Acoustic Realism (No Music)**:
  * This segment completely excludes any music, presenting this historic murder with the coldest, most realistic acoustic environment, avoiding any dramatic or lyrical embellishment.
* **Visceral Audio Details (Physiological Oppression)**:
  * The sound design on the track is highly aggressive. The sticky chewing sound of teeth crushing cartilage and tearing ligaments (Tearing & Chewing of flesh) is clearly captured. The heavy splashing sounds of wading through the stream intertwine with the dull, heavy thuds of the bone club striking One-Ear's body. The overlap of One-Ear's painful, dying whimpers and the heavy impact of the bone club infuses the audience with strong physiological discomfort.

## 4. Editing & Script Adaptation
* **Crisp, Decisive Action Cuts**:
  * During the killing of One-Ear, the editing rhythm is swift and ruthless. Each swing, heavy strike, and One-Ear's collapse are fused together by crisp, decisive cuts, demonstrating the practice of "tool-based violence" as an efficient means of resolution in evolution.
* **Script Adaptation Analysis**:
  * **Comparison with Script Pages 8-9**: In Arthur C. Clarke's original screenplay, Moonwatcher wades across the river carrying the bloody head of a dead lion on a stick ("bloody head of the lion... its fangs displayed") as a totem to psychologically intimidate and scare off his opponents, before beating One-Ear to death with an antelope thigh bone.
  * **Kubrick's Genius De-totemization**: In Kubrick's film, he removes the script detail of carrying the lion's head. This change is extremely sophisticated—a lion's head on a stick acts as a symbol of primitive tribal society, whereas in the film, Moonwatcher's tribe creates massive intimidation solely through "the bone clubs in their hands" and "their steady strides." This focuses the core evolutionary lens entirely on "wielding the tool/weapon itself."
  * **Symmetrical Echo of Evolutionary Violence**: This watering hole kill is a symmetrical echo of the previous "death of Big Tooth" (Shots #035-#044). The first kill was an accidental slamming of the opponent's head onto the ground, relying entirely on beast-like physical wrestling. This time, it is the skilled wielding of a bone club to execute a precise, tool-based murder. This comparison of the two kills visually presents the qualitative evolutionary leap of human ancestors from "instinctual animal conflict" to "tool-based warfare and murder," providing the most abundant narrative and psychological momentum for the next shot (Shot #104) where Moonwatcher throws the bone into the sky.
