# Audiovisual Analysis of Shots #476 to #495: The Visual Revolution of the Stargate Opening, Slit-scan Light Corridors, and the Non-gravitational Perceptual Void

This analysis note integrates shots #476 to #495 into a grouped study. This sequence of shots represents the stunning commencement of the "Stargate" sequence, one of the most famous surrealist visual milestones in film history. As Dave Bowman's EVA Pod reaches the geometric barrier of the Monolith, cosmic space suddenly tears open in silence, and Bowman—along with the audience—is swept into an infinite-dimensional tunnel composed of high-speed, flowing colored geometric light. Through single-point perspective subjective POV shots, the pioneering Slit-scan cinematography developed by Douglas Trumbull, highly saturated psychedelic spectra, and the sharp vocal climax of György Ligeti's *Requiem*, Kubrick presents a thorough deconstruction and reorganization of the human sensory order, transforming space exploration into an unparalleled perceptual revolution.

---

## 1. Composition & Cinematography

* **One-Point Perspective POV & Extreme Depth Extension**:
  * At the moment of entering the Stargate, the camera's perspective switches to an absolute **first-person POV**. Two towering, infinitely tall walls of colored light suddenly rise on both sides of the screen, converging toward a vanishing point at the geometric center of the frame.
  * This single-point perspective composition firmly anchors the audience's visual focus to the geometric center of the frame. As the light erupts from the vanishing point at superluminal speed, rushing past the lens and escaping toward the frame's edges, it generates an intense sensation of being sucked forward and physically falling.
  * This breaks the traditional boundaries of depth of field in cinema. Here, space is infinitely stretched, and the relationship between the horizon, foreground, and background is completely destroyed. It is replaced by a non-Euclidean geometric corridor void of gravity and physical boundaries, consisting solely of geometric rays, giving the audience a physical illusion of bodily weightlessness.
* **Hyper-speed Motion & Perceptual Barrier**:
  * Camera movement here is not traditional panning or tracking, but an extreme abstraction of speed. The flow of light sweeps past the lens at an incredibly high velocity, leaving behind elongated trails of color and persistence of vision on the retina due to the hyper-speed movement.
  * This super-sensory experience of speed visually constructs a "Perceptual Barrier." The camera is no longer a medium for recording reality, but an active force field warping time and space, pushing the spectator's senses to their breaking point.

---

## 2. Color & Lighting Aesthetics

* **Psychedelic Spectral Glow & High-Contrast Void**:
  * These walls of light, generated by the slit-scan technique, display highly saturated colors with an electronic quality—ranging from ionized fluorescent green, fiery orange-red, and deep peacock blue to bright acid yellow. The light is not uniformly distributed but intricately interwoven in precise, regular geometric grids, vertical stripes, and wavy patterns.
  * These vivid light streams erupt against the pitch-black void of deep space, creating a high-intensity chromatic chiaroscuro. Every flicker and fission of the light beams appears remarkably stark against the surrounding darkness.
* **Retinal Persistence & Optical Hypnosis**:
  * The high-intensity fluorescent hues employed by Kubrick leverage the human eye's "Retinal Persistence" effect. As countless high-luminance color beams flicker and flow at high frequencies through the dark, the audience's eyes are unconsciously lulled into a state of "Optical Hypnosis."
  * This is a pure optical experiment. Color here is no longer a narrative ornament, but light waves themselves acting directly upon the viewer's optic nerves, transforming the very "perceptual process of vision" into the transcendental message of the film.

---

## 3. Sound & Music Design

* **Ligeti's Requiem Climax & Acoustic Rupture**:
  * As the Stargate opens and the light corridors erupt, the micropolyphonic chorus of Ligeti's *Requiem* on the soundtrack reaches a terrifying climax.
  * The low, creeping choral textures that previously accompanied the lunar Monolith in Chapter 2 are transformed here into sharp, piercing, high-frequency soprano screaming from dozens of vocal parts simultaneously. This atonal interval and irregular harmony offer no auditory resolution, resembling an endless scream of the soul.
  * This piece of music creates a powerful audio-visual resonance with the fast-flowing walls of light on screen. The "sharpness" of the music perfectly counterpoints the "velocity" of the light, externalizing the physical and mental agony of Bowman being torn apart and reshaped during this dimensional transition into an auditory storm of delirium.
* **Supernatural Noise & Destruction of Vacuum**:
  * Deep in the background of the soundtrack, in addition to the choral voices, low-frequency electronic hums, sounds of gravitational warping, and strange crystalline shattering effects are subtly integrated.
  * This completely obliterates the quiet, regular engine white noise of Chapter 3. The physical vacuum of space is thoroughly torn apart by this supernatural cascade of light and sound, signaling the total suspension of the physical laws of the material universe.

## 4. Editing & Script Adaptation

* **Comparison with Script Page 65: The Sudden End of Script vs. Visual Expansion**:
  * **Original Script**: Arthur C. Clarke's original screenplay and novel ended the entire story on Page 65 with extremely brief words: `In a moment of time, too short to be measured, space turned and twisted upon itself. THE END`. Clarke's writing stopped abruptly here, leaving all subsequent cosmic experiences as literary blank space.
  * **Kubrick's Cinematic Re-creation (Visual Rewriting & Pure Visual Poem)**: In the actual production, Kubrick **completely discarded the setup where the script ended directly on page 65**. Instead, he expanded this brief sentence into one of the greatest, longest, and most ambitious sequences of pure abstract visuals in cinema history. Using nearly 20 minutes of wordless, pure imagery and avant-garde music, he rewrote the meaning of "beyond the infinite," achieving a breathtaking leap from "screenplay text" to "pure cinematic language."
* **Douglas Trumbull's Slit-scan Innovation & Hypnotic Montage**:
  * The realization of this monumental sequence is credited to the **Slit-scan photography technique** invented by special effects master Douglas Trumbull. This technique utilizes a camera that moves back and forth along a track, aimed at a sliding plate containing a tiny vertical slit, behind which colored geometric patterns continuously move. Under long exposure, the camera records the light from the patterns as glowing walls of infinite depth during its forward and backward movement.
  * In terms of editing, Kubrick deliberately stretched the duration of these shots (for example, shot #483 lasts 32 seconds, shot #489 lasts 44 seconds, and shot #495 lasts 22 seconds). Rather than employing the rapid-fire cuts typical of action movies, he uses an extremely slow, patient editing rhythm, allowing these high-brightness, highly saturated streams of light to flicker continuously on screen.
  * This editing rhythm of long takes forces the viewer's perceptual system to immerse itself completely in the physical bombardment of light, elevating what could have been a banal showcase of sci-fi special effects into a transcendental baptism that ultimately deconstructs the order of time and space, bodily boundaries, and human rationality.
